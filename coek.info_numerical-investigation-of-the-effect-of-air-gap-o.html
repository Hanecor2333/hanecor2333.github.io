Numerical model for heat transfer through air gap in thermal protective clothing was coupled with bio-heat transfer model for multi-layer human skin. Coupled CFD-radiation heat transfer model was used for heat transfer through air gap and Pennes' bio-heat transfer model was used for heat transfer through human skin. First and second degree skin burn times were calculated using Henriques' burn integral. Effect of air gap width and air gap orientations were analyzed on heat transfer through air gap and skin burn injuries. Significant impact of air gap width and air gap orientations were observed in first and second degree burn times. First and second degree burn times and hence the thermal protection were found to be higher in case of vertical air gap orientations as compared to horizontal air gap orientations. Air gap orientation influences the thermal protection more at higher air gap widths. Analysis were also performed for determining outcome of heterogeneous air gaps on heat transfer through air gap and protective performance. Heterogeneous air gap cases results in more heat transfer and lower thermal protection as compared to homogeneous air gap cases for both the air gap orientations. Further, effect of fold aspect ratio was also analyzed on first and second degree burn times in case of the heterogeneous air gap.

  1. Introduction


    Thermal protective clothing is essential for firefighters, industrial furnace operators and military personnel as they are usually exposed to extreme hot environments [1]. The external environment can be flame exposure, radiant heat exposure or combined convective/radiant heat exposure. Thermal protective clothing acts as a thermal barrier between external environment and human body by avoiding direct contact of hot environment with the body as shown in Fig. 1. Thermal protective clothing is a multi-layer arrangement with outer shell, moisture barrier and inner liner. Air gap present between clothing and body as well as air gap present in between various fabric layers positively affect protective performance of clothing. Fabrics used for thermal protective clothing does not melt or drip and maintains its mechanical and insulative properties even at high temperatures encountered during various heat exposures. It is still not possible to avoid skin burn injuries for more than few seconds even with the existing thermal

Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analysis were also performed for full scale flame manikins [2]. One of the initial attempt to model heat transfer through air gap was by Torvi [11] and Torvi and Dale [12]. Simple correlation was used to deal with convective heat transfer across the air gap where convection and radiation across air gap were considered to be uncoupled. Above simplified model was modified later [13] to take into account temperature variation along the plane of fabric observed during experimental measurement. Accordingly, treatment of radiation and convection heat transfer were modified to incorporate local temperature variation. Ghazy and Bergstrom [14] analyzed one-dimensional coupled conduction-radiation heat transfer through air gap and neglected convection contribution. A more involved three-dimensional numerical model was later developed by Talukdar et al. [15] where coupled conduction, convection and radiation was considered. Accuracy of the model was not good at higher air gap width and it was improved by Udayraj et al. [16] by treating the natural convection across the air gap in a better way. They [16] developed a coupled CFD-radiation model, accuracy of which was found to be far better than the previous model [15] for various air gap widths. It can be observed from the literature that the extent of air gap at various locations in the body depends on various factors like body posture, body movement, fabric drapability, garment fit, and garment type [17,18]. In actual conditions, air gap available between clothing and body is not uniform as shown in Fig. 1 also. However, in all the previous studies on heat transfer through air gap mentioned above, air gap was considered to be homogeneous or uniform. Also, only horizontal air gaps were considered in all of the above studies. Even the bench top tests based on ISO 9151, ASTM D 4108 and ISO 17492, ASTM F 2700, ASTM F 2703 consider only homogeneous and horizontal orientation of air gap. Only very recently, Udayraj et al. [16] analyzed effect of horizontal and vertical air gaps on heat transfer through air gap and fluid motion in air gap. Even in this study [16] also, only homogeneous air gap was considered. Although a study [17] is available where effect of heterogeneous or wavy air gap was analyzed experimentally, but this

    study was not at all related to thermal protective clothing.

    So, it is clear that the heterogeneous air gap and vertically oriented air gap are the two conditions which exists in most of the practical situations. It is important to consider these two kind of air gaps while analyzing heat transfer through thermal protective clothing. No study is available in the literature where effect of heterogeneous air gap, which is more close to reality, were analyzed on heat transfer through air gap. Hence, in the present study, effect of heterogeneous air gap was analyzed on heat transfer

    through air gap and skin burn injuries for the first time. Analysis was carried out for both the horizontal and vertical air gap orientations.


  2. Problem description


    Present work deals with heat transfer through thermal protective clothing and determining second degree burn time. The problem of heat transfer through thermal protective clothing can be represented with the help of schematic shown in Fig. 2. It can be observed that the problem can be divided into three major subproblems: heat transfer through fabric, heat transfer through air gap and heat transfer through human skin. Present study focusses mainly on heat transfer through the air gap and human skin. Regions of interest here (air gap and human skin layers) are shown with the dotted enclosure in Fig. 2.

    Heat transfer through air gap was analyzed first considering coupled CFD-radiation heat transfer. Air gap was modeled as three-dimensional enclosure with sensor at the top and shim stock at the bottom as shown in Fig. 3 (a). Based on this analysis, variation of total heat flux reached at the sensor with time was obtained. This total heat flux was then applied at the boundary of multi-layer human skin as shown in Fig. 3 (b). Heat transfer through skin layers was analyzed using the Pennes bio-heat equation. With the determined temperature distribution across various skin layers, second degree burn time was then calculated using the Henriques' burn integral.

3.3. Skin burn prediction


Temperature distribution obtained in the skin layers from the skin heat transfer model was used further to analyze skin burn. Skin burn is usually represented quantitatively in terms of degree of burn and classified as first degree burn, second degree burn and third degree burn. Higher degree of burn represents more severe burn. Henriques' burn integral was used to determine first and second degree burn time.

4. Solution procedure


Heat transfer through air gap was analyzed by solving the governing continuity, momentum and energy equations using the finite volume method (FVM) based commercial CFD solver, ANSYS FLUENT. ICEM/CFD was used to model the three-dimensional air gap enclosure geometry and for meshing. Time dependent boundary conditions at the bottom of the air gap enclosure and temperature dependent thermo-physical properties were assigned using a user-defined functions (UDFs). DOM was used to solve the RTE. Details regarding the solution methodology for heat transfer through air gap can be found in Ref. [16]. Based on this numerical analysis of heat transfer through the air gap, total heat flux (qtotal) at the copper calorimeter sensor was obtained. Total heat flux (qtotal) consists of both convective and radiative heat flux reaching at the sensor. The time dependent total heat flux (qtotal) was then applied

air gap width, air gap orientation and heterogeneous air gap were analyzed on skin burn injuries. A recently developed and validated coupled CFD-radiation model was used to analyze heat transfer through air gap along with skin bio-heat transfer model to analyze heat transfer through human skin. In this section, dependence of skin burn injuries on the air gap width, air gap orientation and heterogeneous air gap are presented and discussed.


    1. Effect of heterogeneous air gap


      Depending upon the body posture and body location, air gap between clothing and human body can be either horizontally or vertically orientated. Also, depending upon the body posture, garment fit and fabric drapability, fabric/clothing can be arranged in wavy form or wavy folds of different type. Wavy folds in fabrics may also occur due to thermal shrinkage which is significant where fabrics are subjected to high heat exposures as in the present case. The wavy form or wavy folds present in the garment results in heterogeneous air gap as shown in Fig. 4 (b). In the present section, effect of heterogeneous air gap was analyzed on skin burn for both the air gap orientations and various air gap widths. Homogeneous (flat) and heterogeneous (wavy) air gaps considered in the present study are shown in Fig. 4 (a) and Fig. 4 (b), respectively. Aspect ratio of the wavy folds (ratio of width to height of folds, L/H) was taken as

    2. Effect of orientation and air gap width


      Effect of air gap width and orientation for the homogeneous and heterogeneous air gaps are shown in Fig. 7. It can be observed from Fig. 7 (a) and Fig. 7 (b) that both the first and second degree burn

      times depend significantly on the air gap width and air gap orientation. Both the first and second degree burn time increases as air gap width increases. This is due to the fact that the total and radiative heat fluxes reaching at the sensor decreases significantly as air gap width increases [16]. It means that higher air gap widths results in higher thermal protection. Skin burn time for the vertical air gap orientations were found to be higher than the skin burn time for the horizontal air gap orientation for all the air gaps as shown in Fig. 7. This is due to significantly lower total heat flux obtained at the sensor for the vertical air gap orientation as

      compared to the horizontal air gap orientation as evident from Fig. 6. It was observed in previous study [16] also that for any given air gap width, the vertical air gap orientation results in lower total heat flux and convective heat flux at the sensor as compared to the horizontal air gap orientation.

      Effect of heterogeneity in the air gap is small as compared to the effect of orientations and width of the air gap. Among the three main parameters studied, effect of the air gap width was found largest on skin burn times followed by other two parameters, air gap orientation and heterogeneity in the air gap. It can also be observed that the effect of air gap orientation on both the first and second degree burn times increased as the air gap width increased for the homogeneous as well as heterogeneous air gap cases. However, for the heterogeneous air gap case, effect of the air gap orientation at lower air gap widths was found negligible. This can be observed from Fig. 7 (a) and Fig. 7 (b) for both the first and second degree burn times. Effect of air gap width was also evident on the relative difference between the first and second degree burn times. For any given air gap orientation (horizontal or vertical) and air gap type (homogeneous and heterogeneous), first degree burn time was observed significantly lower than the second degree burn time for higher air gap width (say 19.1 mm) cases. The difference between the first degree burn time and the second degree burn time decreases as air gap width decreases.


    3. Effect of fold aspect ratio


Distribution of air gap throughout the human body varies significantly depending upon the body posture, garment fit, garment style and body movement [17,18]. So, depending upon the situation, the heterogeneous air gap considered above can be of different type. The aspect ratio and height of the folds may change. Even the fold shape can also be of different type. In the present section, effect of various aspect ratio of folds (L/H) for the case of heterogeneous air gap is analyzed on skin burn injuries.

In the previous sections, aspect ratio, L/H = 3, was considered and it was selected based on the previous study [17]. It can be observed from Figs. 9 and 10 that decreasing the L/H below 3 did not affect the first and second degree burn time in case of 19.1 mm air gap of horizontal as well as vertical orientations. However, the first and second degree burn times decreased slightly when the aspect ratio decreased from 3 to 2 in case of 6.4 mm air gaps for both the horizontal and vertical air gap orientations. This is due to the fact that the convective heat flux at the sensor increased when fold aspect ratio reduced from 3 to 2 as shown in Fig.12 (c). Although the radiant heat flux are almost same for both the aspect ratios as shown in Fig.12 (b), higher convective heat flux for L/H = 2 results in higher

total heat flux as compared to L/H = 3 case as shown in Fig.12 (a). So, the higher total heat flux in case of L/H = 2 is the reason of lower first and second degree burn times as compared to the case of L/H = 3 for

6.4 mm air gap.

On the other hand, increasing the aspect ratio above 3 results in significant decrease in both the first and second degree burn times for all the air gap widths and air gap orientations considered in the present study. This is due to increase in radiant heat flux at the sensor for the case of L/H = 5 as shown in Fig. 11 (b) and Fig. 12 (b) for 19.1 mm and 6.4 mm vertically orientated air gaps, respectively. It causes increase in the total heat flux as evident from Fig. 11 (a) and Fig. 12 (a). It is important to note that for all the heterogeneous air gap cases of different aspect ratios, the first and second degree burn times are found to be lower than the homogeneous air gap (L/ H = ∞) case. It means that in case of the heterogeneous air gap, skin is more susceptible to burn injuries. Further, it is clearly evident from Figs. 9 and 10 that the effect of air gap orientation (horizontal or vertical) is more pronounced in case of higher air gap widths (19.1 mm). It is clear from the significant differences in the first and second degree burn times observed between the horizontal and vertical air gap cases for 19.1 mm air gap width.

Effect of fold aspect ratio was also analyzed for the heterogeneous air gap cases. Following are some of the major conclusions that can be drawn from the study:


    • Heterogeneous air gap results in more heat transfer across air gaps for both horizontal and vertical air gaps of different widths. Consequently, heterogeneous air gap cases results in lower first and second degree burn times as compared to the homogeneous air gap cases. It means skin is more susceptible to burn injuries in case of the heterogeneous air gaps.

    • First and second degree burn time increases significantly as the air gap width increases for both the air gap orientations and homogeneous as well as heterogeneous air gaps.

    • Vertical air gap orientation offers better thermal protection as compared to the horizontal air gap orientation for homogeneous and heterogeneous air gaps of different widths.

    • Effect of air gap orientations on first and second degree burn times are found to be more significant at higher air gap widths as compared to lower air gap widths for both the homogeneous and heterogeneous air gaps.

    • Among heterogeneous air gap, air gap width and air gap orientation; effect of air gap width is maximum on first and second degree burn times followed by the effects of air gap orientation and heterogeneity in the air gaps.

    • For both the horizontal and vertical air gap orientations, decreasing the fold aspect ratio (L/H) below 3 results in decrease in the first and second degree burn times for 6.4 mm air gap whereas burn times does not change for 19.1 mm air gap.

    • First and second degree burn times decrease significantly as fold aspect ratio increases above 3 for both the orientations and various air gap widths.


It is observed from the present study that apart from air gap width, air gap orientations and heterogeneous air gap plays significant role in heat transfer through air gaps and thermal protective performance of clothing. The existing International standards for determining thermal protective performance of fabrics using various bench top test does not take these effects into account. Effects of these parameters should be considered in future for more realistic thermal protective performance assessment of clothing.
